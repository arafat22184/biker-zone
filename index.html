Frequently Asked Questions

What are the most important things I should know about riding a bike?

Safety First! Always obey the rules of the road. Obey all traffic signals, signs, and laws. Get in the mindset of “driving” your bike—not just “riding” your bike. This will help you be a more focused and legally compliant bike rider.

How can I tell if my helmet is old and I need a new one?

Helmets are crucial for safety, and they do have a lifespan. Here’s how you can tell if your helmet needs to be replaced:

1. Age of the Helmet Most helmet manufacturers recommend replacing helmets every 3–5 years, even if they haven't been in an accident. The materials degrade over time due to exposure to UV rays, sweat, and general wear and tear. Check the manufacturing date inside the helmet (usually on a sticker).

2. Visible Damage Inspect the outer shell for cracks, dents, or abrasions. Look inside for crushed foam, cracks, or separation in the inner lining.

3. Fit and Comfort If the helmet feels loose, wobbly, or doesn't fit snugly anymore, it’s likely stretched or worn out. Check the padding—if it’s compressed or deteriorating, it’s time for a new one.

4. Chin Strap and Fasteners Ensure the straps are still sturdy and functional. Frayed or weak straps compromise safety.

5. Past Impacts If the helmet has been involved in an accident or a significant impact, replace it immediately. Even if it looks fine externally, the inner foam may have been compromised.

6. Technology and Standards Older helmets may not meet current safety standards (e.g., DOT, ECE, or Snell certifications). Newer models often have improved materials and safety features.

If you’re uncertain, it’s better to err on the side of caution and replace the helmet—your safety is worth it!

My bike has been in storage is it safe to ride?

If your bike has been in storage for a while, it’s important to do a thorough check before riding to ensure it’s safe and functional. Here’s a step-by-step guide:

1. Inspect the Tires Check for cracks, dry rot, or flat spots: Tires can degrade over time, especially if stored improperly or exposed to extreme temperatures. Test air pressure: Inflate to the recommended PSI. Old tires may not hold air well. Look at the tread: Worn or cracked tread needs replacing.

2. Examine the Chain Check for rust or stiffness: Clean the chain with a degreaser and lubricate it. If it’s excessively corroded, replace it. Test movement: Spin the pedals and ensure the chain moves smoothly over the gears.

3. Brakes Inspect brake pads: Look for wear or hardening. Replace if they’re too thin or cracked. Test brake levers: Pull the levers to ensure they engage properly. If they feel spongy, bleed the brake lines (for hydraulic brakes) or tighten cables (for mechanical brakes). Check rotors or rims: Ensure no warping or damage.

4. Suspension and Frame Look for cracks or damage: Check the frame, fork, and suspension components for any visible issues. Test suspension: Compress and release the suspension to ensure it works smoothly without squeaks or resistance.

5. Lights and Electronics Test all lights: Headlights, taillights, and indicators need to work. Battery condition: For motorcycles, check the battery’s charge and terminals. Recharge or replace if necessary.

6. Fuel and Fluids (for Motorcycles) Drain old fuel: Stale fuel can damage the engine. Refill with fresh gasoline. Check oil and coolant levels: Top up or replace fluids as needed. Inspect hoses: Look for cracks, leaks, or brittle rubber.

7. Bolts and Fasteners Tighten everything: Ensure all nuts, bolts, and screws are secure. Check the handlebars: Ensure they’re aligned and firmly attached.

8. Test Ride After everything looks good, take the bike for a slow test ride in a safe area to check brakes, gears, and overall performance.

If you’re unsure or notice major issues, it’s a good idea to take your bike to a professional mechanic for a full safety inspection.

What rules should I follow when riding my bike?

Riding a bike—whether a bicycle or motorcycle—comes with responsibilities to ensure your safety and the safety of others. Here are the key rules and tips to follow:

1. Follow Traffic Laws Obey traffic signals and signs: Stop at red lights and stop signs, and yield when required. Ride in the same direction as traffic: Never ride against the flow. Use proper lanes: Stay in designated bike lanes if available or to the far-right side of the road.

2. Wear the Right Gear Helmet: Always wear a properly fitted helmet. Protective clothing (for motorcycles): Gloves, jackets, pants, and boots designed for riding. Reflective gear: Wear bright or reflective clothing, especially when riding at night or in low visibility.

3. Be Visible Use lights: Front white lights and rear red lights or reflectors are essential for night riding. Signal your intentions: Use hand signals for bicycles or indicator lights for motorcycles when turning or changing lanes. Avoid blind spots: Stay visible to drivers, especially trucks and buses.

4. Stay Alert Watch for hazards: Look out for potholes, debris, wet roads, and uneven surfaces. Avoid distractions: Don’t use your phone or listen to loud music while riding. Anticipate drivers’ actions: Make eye contact with drivers when possible to ensure they see you.

5. Control Your Speed Ride at a safe speed: Adjust your speed to match road and weather conditions. Follow speed limits: Especially in residential areas or zones with heavy pedestrian traffic.

6. Maintain Safe Distance Keep a buffer: Maintain enough space between you and vehicles in front of you. Pass safely: Overtake only when it’s safe, and avoid weaving between vehicles.

7. Use Signals and Communicate Hand signals for cyclists: Left turn: Left arm straight out. Right turn: Right arm straight out (or left arm bent upwards). Stopping: Left arm bent downwards. Indicators for motorcyclists: Always use turn signals to communicate.

8. Respect Pedestrians Yield to pedestrians: Stop at crosswalks and give way when they have the right of way. Don’t ride on sidewalks: Unless permitted.

9. Know and Prepare for Weather Rain: Reduce speed, avoid sudden braking, and watch for slippery surfaces. Wind: Brace for gusts, especially on open roads. Night: Use proper lighting and reduce speed for better reaction time.

10. Regular Bike Maintenance Check before riding: Inspect tires, brakes, chain, and lights. Service regularly: For motorcycles, keep fluids, engine, and suspension in good condition.

Bonus: Ride Responsibly Avoid aggressive riding or risky behavior like wheelies or sudden swerving. Never ride under the influence of alcohol or drugs. Always carry your license, registration, and insurance (for motorcycles). By following these rules, you’ll ensure a safer and more enjoyable ride!
